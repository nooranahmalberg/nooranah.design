Minimising risk to safety for veterinarians

Role

I joined Covetrus in 2023. My first task as a UX designer on the product team was to design the second most requested feature on Pendo: more obvious patient medical and behavioural alerts for veterinary staff.

Problem

When I dug into this problem, I realised that veterinarians were facing multiple issues because of the lack of prominence of these alerts. The most significant problem was that staff were missing the patient’s behavioural status on the system and getting hurt in appointments. The second problem was that multiple patients with behavioural issues were being booked in for a visit at the same time, which could pose a risk to everyone’s safety and caused disruptions in the clinic.

Method

We interviewed a total of 4 veterinary staff regarding their usage and thoughts on behavioural alerts as implemented on both Ascend (cloud-based) and RxWorks (server-based). 2 interviews occurred during my clinic visits while the other 2 were done remotely.

Veterinarians are busy

When it comes to research, it’s typically recommended to aim for 5-6 participants per user group but veterinary staff are quite busy people as they manage demands of a clinic. I made a Loom video to collect feedback on designs or consent for an interview from 11 users who voted on the Pendo request, but received no views. (If you have any little tidbit on what to do instead, I’d love to know!)

Identifying key points in their workflow

Clinic visits were particularly helpful in illuminating how the software was used to communicate with staff across the clinic. A receptionist checking in a patient would use Ascend to send notes to a veterinarian awaiting their next patient in a treatment room. On the platform, I identified each area in the main workflow where the patient’s behaviour status could be more obvious: Client check-in, outpatients board, inpatients board and patient details page.

One customer's impact on scope

Well, that was the plan until we received an impassioned email from a customer. They were one straw away from switching over to another practice management system because two “aggressive” patients had arrived at their clinic at the same time. This problem was echoed in past interview sessions and their pain struck a chord in our hearts. I expanded my scope to include the areas where receptionists would book in patients: Appointments calendar and booking flow.

Tons of information at a glance

After a bit of exploration, the senior designer and I landed on an idea that would deliver a ton of detail in a compact form. One key insight from all interviews was that behaviour notes were crucial in giving more context around a patient’s behaviour status. For example, a “care required” dog may only be sensitive to other dogs and not affected by people. This is why the alerts chips try to display as much of that information at a glance. Since the findability of the alerts and notes was low before, I brought it up to the top of the page and incorporated it with the patient header component. Using colour, iconography and copy to flag for the user’s attention.

Outcome

I shared designs with Covetrus team members who have veterinary experience as well as customers. Overall, the final designs tested favourably among them. Since my time at Covetrus was brief, I can’t share any ongoing metrics on how the solution is performing. But if I could, the usage of the behaviour status field would be an excellent measure since a large percentage of patients are currently “not assessed”. A rise in patients being labelled with a behaviour status may indicate that the alerts are more prominent.
