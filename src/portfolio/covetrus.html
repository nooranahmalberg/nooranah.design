Understanding the problem

On Ascend, there were numerous user-submitted Pendo reports about patient behaviour alerts and their lack of prominence. It became the second most requested UX improvement at the time. As a UX designer, I investigated these requests by interviewing different roles in a veterinary clinic from clinic owners to receptionists, and observing how they used Ascend when a patient visited their clinic.

During the clinic visit, I observed that vets relied on the software to share information with one another, rather than a verbal exchange. The UI was quite dense in information so it was easy to miss a small badge that signalled patient behaviour. Not realising that a patient was prone to aggression led to accidents and stress in the clinic.

I learnt that aggression was not the only type of behaviour vets were concerned about. The interaction between behaviours (e.g. a nervous pet and a pet prone to barking in the same room) and the detail about the pet (e.g. Only nervous around women) were just as important as the behaviour label themselves.

From the initial research, these were the two problems we were trying to solve around unclear patient behavioural alerts:

  • Receptionists are booking in patients with conflicting behaviours for appointments at the same time
  • The veterinarian in the consult room are missing critical updates to a patient's behaviour and notes

Ideating and Prototyping

I first started on some ideas for My Day. Since I was determined to make it really obvious, my initial inspiration were amber alerts and medical sirens to draw the user's attention.

(Include gif) Obviously, this was far too distracting to be useful for receptionists... So I toned it back with borders around the patient picture. It drew attention to the area and was discreet enough to explain away to pet parents.

(Include kanban board) Yes, one user need I later discovered was to avoid any unnecessary offense to patient guardians who may glance at the screen by chance. This meant no overt "DANGER" labels around pet pictures. I thankfully learnt this from feedback by my colleagues with experience in the field. They were really helpful during this project since it can be difficult to get a hold of veterinary staff who are usually very busy.

With the patient information display, there was already a warning banner that appeared above the consultation view. In my concepts, I included that same banner across the platform to reuse an existing design pattern. As I was sharing these designs, we realised that we wanted to take the opportunity to improve the warning banner and bring in greater consistency with how patient information is displayed. So I collaborated with the senior UX designer to create a global patient info component with a minimise/expand interaction.

Lessons learnt

The work behind this feature improvement was highly iterative. New problems emerged along the way and solutions often changed with each new round of feedback, which is what being in an agile team is all about.

But what started as a Pendo request felt like a mega-feature that addressed every problem imaginable relating to patient alerts. We even managed to include improvements to the calendar app in the feature release to address a well-timed customer complaint.

I could have worked more closely with product managers and engineers on how to stagger the problems and prioritise work for release.
